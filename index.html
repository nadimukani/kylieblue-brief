The smallest details. Easy ones to knock out.

These tiny strings of text — a handle, an email, a name — are what the internet uses to recognize you. Pick the ones you want to be found by for the next decade.

You have great taste. Trust it. Drop in five to fifteen things that make you say "yes, like that" — they don't have to be photography. A magazine spread, a hotel lobby, a film still, a typeface — all useful. We'll read your taste exactly.
Drop or click. Images get resized for preview — full quality stays on your computer.

The strongest About pages don't sell. They sound exactly like the person who wrote them. Don't try to be impressive — be specific. Specific is what people remember ten years later.

Six

What you offer, and what it costs

Even rough drafts move things forward.

Your prices teach clients what your work is worth. If the number scares you a little, write it down anyway — half the work of pricing is just saying it out loud. You can revise. You can't revise what you never named.
Name each (give them voice — "The Half-Day," "The Whole Story" beats "Package 1") and list what's inside (hours, edited images, second shooter, prints, gallery delivery time).
How travel works (flat / per mile / included within X miles), and the deposit % to book.
Engagement session, rehearsal, second shooter, prints, an album.
Seven

How clients reach you

Every word on your inquiry form teaches the client how to write to you.

The right questions filter the right people. Asking for a budget range gently teaches "this is a real conversation, not a window-shop." Asking how they found you teaches you which marketing actually works.
Name and email are always there.
A few lines so they know you got it. Rough draft is plenty.
Eight

The questions you get asked

If you've answered the same one three times in DMs, it belongs here.

FAQs aren't customer service. They're a chance to be funny, warm, and clear in a way nothing else on the site can be. The more "you" the answers sound, the more they convert. Skip the corporate voice — write like you'd text a friend.
Format any way that works — Q on one line, A on the next, or just write them as a list.

Quote, name, occasion. We'll format.
Anywhere your work has been written up — with links if you have them.

Ten

What you want, and don't

Saying what you don't want is often the fastest path to what you do.

You'll be surprised how clarifying it is to write down the things you hate. Every "no" you say here makes the design sharper. The bad photographer websites are bad because nobody told them what they didn't want.
Autoplay music, video backgrounds, popups, heavy animation — name them.
"Capturing memories," "timeless," "storyteller" — name what you never want on your site.
