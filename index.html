A handful of soft questions to help us build your website. No rush, no rules.

Hi my love,

This isn't a form. There's no right answer — a word, a screenshot, a feeling is enough. Skip what doesn't speak to you. Come back to it whenever you want.

You can leave any time. Your answers stay safe right here, waiting for you. Nothing is permanent. Nothing is graded.

Take your time. This is going to be beautiful — like you.

Whenever you're ready.

One

Who you are, in your own words

Don't overthink. First instinct is almost always the truest one.

Picture your favorite photo you've ever taken. The light, the moment, the person in it. Hold it in your head and answer with that photo as your witness — your answers will be truer.
"I'm a [city] [type] photographer who [the thing that makes you, you]." Use your real voice.
Not what sounds nice — what's actually true.
A different feeling from your photographs — the site has its own atmosphere.
Two or three sentences. The kind of person you want filling your inbox.
It's okay to name it — saying no helps the site say it for you, gently.
Two

What you're known by

The smallest details. Easy ones to knock out.

These tiny strings of text — a handle, an email, a name — are what the internet uses to recognize you. Pick the ones you want to be found by for the next decade.
Pinterest, TikTok, anywhere. Optional.
Three

The look — show, don't tell

The fastest way to design something beautiful is to show it.

You have great taste. Trust it. Drop in five to fifteen things that make you say "yes, like that" — they don't have to be photography. A magazine spread, a hotel lobby, a film still, a typeface — all useful. We'll read your taste exactly.
Anything that makes you say "yes, like that." Images get resized for preview — full quality stays on your computer.
Two or three. Famous or unknown — doesn't matter.
A brand, a hotel, a magazine, a film, a song. Anything.
Sites you'd hate to be compared to. Sometimes "not that" is the clearest answer.
Just describe them in your own words.
Four

Your photographs

You have these already. The hard part is choosing fewer than feels natural.

Awwwards-winning sites all share one thing — restraint. Your strongest 30 are stronger than 200. The fewer images you show, the more weight each one carries. Every cut makes the site stronger.
If a stranger saw only this, it would tell them exactly who you are.
Not a selfie. We want people to see your face.
30–50 of your strongest. Mix of vertical + horizontal, mix of wide + detail. Dropbox, Drive, WeTransfer — whatever you use.
Weddings / Couples / Families / Editorial — whatever yours are. Use the names you want on the site.
Five

Your story

Don't write copy. Just answer. We'll shape your voice into the page.

The strongest About pages don't sell. They sound exactly like the person who wrote them. Don't try to be impressive — be specific. Specific is what people remember ten years later.
From the client's perspective — what's the experience like?
Your dog, where you grew up, the album on repeat, the weird thing you collect.
How you learned. Where you've been featured. Anything you're proud of.
Six

What you offer, and what it costs

Even rough drafts move things forward.

Your prices teach clients what your work is worth. If the number scares you a little, write it down anyway — half the work of pricing is just saying it out loud. You can revise. You can't revise what you never named.
Name each one with some voice — "The Half-Day," "The Whole Story" beats "Package 1." List what's inside (hours, edited images, second shooter, prints, delivery time).
How travel works (flat / per mile / included within X miles) and the deposit % to book.
Engagement session, rehearsal, second shooter, prints, an album.
Seven

How clients reach you

Every word on your inquiry form teaches the client how to write to you.

The right questions filter the right people. Asking for a budget range gently teaches "this is a real conversation, not a window-shop." Asking how they found you teaches you which marketing actually works.
Name and email are always there.
A few lines, in your voice, so they know you got it. Rough draft is plenty.
Eight

The questions you get asked

If you've answered the same one three times in DMs, it belongs here.

FAQs aren't customer service. They're a chance to be funny, warm, and clear in a way nothing else on the site can be. The more "you" the answers sound, the more they convert. Skip the corporate voice — write like you'd text a friend.
Up to five Q&A pairs. Format any way that works — just a list is fine.
Nine

What people say about you

Real words from real clients carry more weight than any tagline.

A screenshot of a sweet text from a bride is enough — we'll set it. The shorter and more specific the testimonial, the more it lands. "She made my mom feel beautiful for the first time in twenty years" beats "highly recommend!!" every time.
Three to seven. A quote, a name, when. We'll format.
Magazines, blogs, features — links if you have them.
Venues, planners, brands. Only if it adds something.
Ten

What you want, and don't

Saying what you don't want is often the fastest path to what you do.

You'll be surprised how clarifying it is to write down the things you hate. Every "no" you say here makes the design sharper. The bad photographer websites are bad because nobody told them what they didn't want.
Autoplay music, video backgrounds, popups, heavy animation — name them.
"Capturing memories," "timeless," "storyteller" — anything that makes you cringe.
Eleven

When

A real date — even a soft one — is the difference between "someday" and "happening."

Pick the date that scares you a little. The one that makes you feel like you have to actually finish your part. Deadlines are gifts — they protect the project from drifting.
A season, a press piece, a wedding on the books, a launch.
